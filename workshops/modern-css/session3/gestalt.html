The Gestalt Principles

The German word 'Gestalt' means 'shape' or 'form'. At the beginning of the twentieth century a number of psychologists, started to look at the way we perceive the world, and defined several interrelated concepts into a cohesive theory. As designing has matured, designers have looked to psychology to help to understand how the design of something might be inteprited by an individual. Now many designers reference and refer to the Gestalt Principles to help shape their compositions.

Gestalt can also be thought of as 'unified whole' or as the whole is more than the sum of the parts. It is sometimes worded as the whole is different from the sum of its parts.

The Principles of Gestalt describes the general tendancies for human perception of objects and groupings. It is a mixture of Pychology and Perceptive sciences and design principles. Due to the nature of the Gestalt Principles, it can be difficult to talk about one without talking about others.

Google search for Gestalt Principles will result in a lot of confussion about how many there are and what they are called. This is in part due to modern thinking expanding the original principles and others trying to clarify the principles. In this presentation, I have just chosen some of the most popular names for each principle, and added others to the list where I found that one website mentions something a little bit differently to another.

It is important realise that most of the words below are only one side of a two sided coin (so to speak). For example, if we talk about concepts like Similarity, Proximity and Symmetry, we can also talk about the opposite of the concept: dissimilar, distance and asymmetry. Just because this list contains one of the words does not mean that the opposite is bad.

Similarity

The Law of Similarity shows that the brain tends to group objects together if they share the some characteristics. This may be colour, shape, style, size, it could be based on alignment or even location.

The brain will also add things to multiple groups automatically. Continuation

Continuity is a principle that works on the minds tendancy to follow a line. It is in part about alignment but might also be orientation or orchestration, in the example below can you see the green boxes form both rows and columns, they help to draw the eye across the page and down.

Another example of this is where Figure / Ground (see below) is used to draw the eye across the page.

The idea of guiding the eye has lead web designer to define three basic models for websites. When looking at a particular website it may not for into one of those patterns. Also as they are basic, there may be others that I have not found yet in my travels.

Note that continuation can be used to relate topics that "break" other principles, for example, you could create two groups of words or images where each item in the group is close to each others (implying a connect between them) then you could add a line from one element to an element in the other group (implying a different connection), each connection may be a different way to link the topics.

The Amazon logo shows a different use of this principle, expressing the idea that at Amazon you can get anything from A to Z.

Closure

Closure is an interesting one as it's about what is not there. Look at the example below, is there a white rectangle in the middle? The Law of Closure, states that human brain has a tendancy to fill in the blanks.

A well known logo that has been made using the closure principle is the World Wildlife Fund's image. Is it a panda or is it just black blobs in a pattern? If you place the logo on a black backgound, you can see that there are two white blobs too.

The Fedex logo is another example, can you see the white arrow formed by the "E" and "x".

The principle of figure / ground is related to the Law of Closure, it is the work the mind does to understand a recognisable but incomplete image. It can be seen in many logos, like the bear in the mountain on the Toblarone, the arrow in the FedEx logo,

From one viewpoint, the figure is foreground, while the ground is the scene or background in which the figure "stands". But with a small shift in perspective and the ground jumps out and becomes a new subject while the figure drops back to form a new background.
