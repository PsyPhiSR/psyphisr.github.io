Gestalt Principles

The German word 'Gestalt' means 'shape' or 'form'. At the beginning of the twentieth century a number of psychologists, started to look at the way we perceive the world, and defined several interrelated concepts into a cohesive theory. As designing has matured, designers have looked to psychology to help to understand how the design of something might be inteprited by an individual. Now many designers reference and refer to the Gestalt Principles to help shape their compositions.

Gestalt can also be thought of as 'unified whole' or as the whole is more than the sum of the parts. It is sometimes worded as the whole is different from the sum of its parts.

The Principles of Gestalt describes the general tendancies for human perception of objects and groupings. It is a mixture of Pychology and Perceptive sciences and design principles. Due to the nature of the Gestalt Principles, it can be difficult to talk about one without talking about others.

Google search for Gestalt Principles will result in a lot of confussion about how many there are and what they are called. This is in part due to modern thinking expanding the original principles and others trying to clarify the principles. In this presentation, I have just chosen some of the most popular names for each principle, and added others to the list where I found that one website mentions something a little bit differently to another.

It is important realise that most of the words below are only one side of a two sided coin (so to speak). For example, if we talk about concepts like Similarity, Proximity and Symmetry, we can also talk about the opposite of the concept: dissimilar, distance and asymmetry. Just because this list contains one of the words does not mean that the opposite is bad.

Continuation

Continuity is a principle that works on the minds tendancy to follow a line. It is in part about alignment but might also be orientation or orchestration, in the example below can you see the green boxes form both rows and columns, they help to draw the eye across the page and down.

Another example of this is where Figure / Ground (see below) is used to draw the eye across the page.

The idea of guiding the eye has lead web designer to define three basic models for websites. When looking at a particular website it may not for into one of those patterns. Also as they are basic, there may be others that I have not found yet in my travels.

Note that continuation can be used to relate topics that "break" other principles, for example, you could create two groups of words or images where each item in the group is close to each others (implying a connect between them) then you could add a line from one element to an element in the other group (implying a different connection), each connection may be a different way to link the topics.

The Amazon logo shows a different use of this principle, expressing the idea that at Amazon you can get anything from A to Z, it also implies that Amazon will make you happy.

In the example above, imagine that the dots are all one colour, you are likely to think that there is one straight dotted line across the page and that is curved. Adding in the colour makes it seem like the lines do not continue as expected.

Closure

Closure is an interesting one as it's about what is not there. Look at the example below, is there a white rectangle in the middle? The Law of Closure, states that human brain has a tendancy to fill in the blanks.

A well known logo that has been made using the closure principle is the World Wildlife Fund's image. Is it a panda or is it just black blobs in a pattern? If you place the logo on a black backgound, you can see that there are two white blobs too.

The Fedex logo is another example, can you see the white arrow formed by the "E" and "x".

Figure / Ground

This principle encompasses both parts of the coin, the backgound (just called groundin this principle), and the foreground (figure or sometimes subject). For this principle, the figure and the ground can be swapped to create something different, a new idea.

There is a famous picture of a vase that demonstrats this principle, or is it a picture of two people facing each other.

The principle of figure / ground is related to the Law of Closure, it is the work the mind does to understand a recognisable but incomplete image. It can be seen in many logos, like the bear in the mountain on the Toblerone, the arrow in the FedEx logo,

From one viewpoint, the figure is foreground, while the ground is the scene or background in which the figure "stands". But with a small shift in perspective and the ground jumps out and becomes a new subject while the figure drops back to form a new background.

Symmetry

If you fold a symmetrical figure in half the two sides will match up. A symmetrical design can often look pleasing to the eye, and is considered static, stable, safe, balanced. Balance or Order are other words that are sometimes used for this principle.

Likewise, asymmetry is often associated with movement, instablity, rocking the boat, edgy. Depending on the brand an asymmetrical design can really punch a message home.

There is a German word used for this principle, prägnanz, the principle of organisation. It states that people tend to perceive forms as the simplest and most meaningful, stable, and complete structures that conditions permit. The word means something like Good figure or pithiness (literally defined as "brief, forceful and meaningful in expression").

Common Fate

This principle is a more recent addition to the Gestalt Principles, it states that people tend to groups things that are point or moving in the same direction.

Other Design Principles

From the above you can see that this is only the beginning and within the the Gestalt Principles I have mentioned a number of other Principles. Let us briefly discuss them here.

Movement

This is the idea that a design can convey a sense of movement or stillness.

Hierarchy

You can find some sort of hierarchy in many design projects, from a simple page or menu structure to the relative sizes of headings.

Proportion

We have a tendancy to think that bigger things are more important. We can use different proportions to draw attention to some details and de-emphasize an element by making it smaller.

Repetition

Repeating an idea or motif can reenforce an idea or brand.

Pattern

As human beings we are good at recognising pattern, think of the times that you have seen a shape of something in the clouds, or a face in leaves of a tree. Designs often use this ability, to spark interest.

Unity

Unity is the idea that everything should work together. A user may be confused if we use designs that say different things unless we do so intentionally.

Variety

Time and time again, we seem to present things that contradict each other. In the last principle, we said a unity and here we are taking about, adding in variations.

Emphasis

Emphasis can be achieved in many ways, typeface, size, colour, position, etc.

Rhythm

This is the idea that a design has a sort of rhythm, similar to the idea of variety, a design can became boring if the same idea is used too consistently. Image a song that only plays one note in the same rhythm all the way through, you might listen to it once but probably not twice.

Balance

We talked about this in the Symmetry principle above.

Contrast

Another word for Variety or Difference.

Alignment

We could make two sets of text align on the same line on a page, where one is within a box when the box is made bigger the the space for the text.
